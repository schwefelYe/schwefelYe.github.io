Mean estimation problem

Consider a random variable XXX that can take values from a finite set of real numbers denoted as X\mathcal{X}X, suppose that our task is to calculate the mean or expected value1^11 of XXX, i.e., E[X]\mathbb{E}[X]E[X].

Two approaches can be used to calculate E[X]\mathbb{E}[X]E[X].

Model based case

The first approach is model-based. Here, the model refers to the probability distribution of XXX. If the model is known, then the mean can be directly calculated based on the definition of the expected value:

E[X]=∑x∈Xp(x)x\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x)x E[X]=x∈X∑​p(x)x

Model free case

The second approach is model-free. When the probability distribution (i.e., the model) of XXX is unknown, suppose that we have some samples {x1,x2,…,xn}\{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\}{x1​,x2​,…,xn​} of XXX. Then, the mean can be approximated as

E[X]≈xˉ≜1n∑j=1nxj.\mathbb{E}[X] \approx \bar{x} \triangleq \frac{1}{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} x_j. E[X]≈xˉ≜n1​j=1∑n​xj​.

When nnn is small, this approximation may not be accurate. However, as nnn increases, the approximation becomes increasingly accurate. When n→∞n \to \inftyn→∞, we have xˉ→E[X]\bar{x} \to \mathbb{E}[X]xˉ→E[X].

This is guaranteed by the law of large numbers (L.L.N.): the average of a large number of samples is close to the expected value.

This is called the Monte Carlo method.

Law of large numbers

For a random variable XXX, suppose that {xi}i=1n\{x_i\}_{i=1}^n{xi​}i=1n​ are some i.i.d. samples. Let xˉ=1n∑i=1nxi\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n x_ixˉ=n1​∑i=1n​xi​ be the average of the samples. Then,

E[xˉ]=E[X],var[xˉ]=1nvar[X].\begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}[\bar{x}] &= \mathbb{E}[X], \\ \text{var}[\bar{x}] &= \frac{1}{n} \text{var}[X]. \end{aligned} E[xˉ]var[xˉ]​=E[X],=n1​var[X].​

The above two equations indicate that xˉ\bar{x}xˉ is an unbiased estimate of E[X]\mathbb{E}[X]E[X], and its variance decreases to zero as nnn increases to infinity.

Iterative mean estimation

There are two methods to compute equation (1). The first method is the non-incremental method, where we have to collect all the samples first and then calculate the average. If the number of samples is large, we may have to wait for a long time until all of the samples are collected.
